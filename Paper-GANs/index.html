Paper-GANs

Author: Louieworth Date: September 24, 2020  20:08:39

Abstract#

There are two models in this framework:

  • A generative model G which captures the data distribution.
  • A discriminative model D which estimates the probability that a sample came from the training data rather than G.

Training process:

  • G is to maximize the probability of D making a mistake.as analogous to a team of counterfeiters, trying to produce fake currency and use it without
  • D learns to determine whether a sample is from the model distribution or the data distribution. As analogous to the police, trying to detect the counterfeit currency.

Introduction#

The promise of deep learning is to discover rich, hierarchical models that represent probability distribution over the kinds of data encountered in AI application. Deep generative models have had less of an impact, due to the difficulty of approximating many intractable probabilistic computations that arise in MLE.

This model generates samples by passing random noise through a multilayer perceptron, and the discriminative model is also a multilayer perceptron. In this case, we can train both models using only back-propagation and dropout algorithms. No approximate inference or Markov chains are necessary.

Adversarial Nets#

$p_g$ is the generator’s distribution over data $x$

$p_z(z)$ is a prior input noise variables

$G(z;\theta_g)$ represents a mapping to data space, G is a differentiable function represented by a multiplayer perceptron with parameter $\theta_g$

$D(x;\theta_d) $ is a second multiplayer perceptron, $D(X)$ represents the probability that x cam e from the data rather than $p_g$

Question#

  1. why we alternate between k steps of optimizing D and one step of optimizing G.
    • Optimizing D to completion in the inner loop of training is computationally prohibitive, and on finite datasets would result in overfitting.
    • This results in D being maintained near its optimal solution, so long as G changes slowly enough. This strategy is analogous to the way that SML/PCD [31, 29] training maintains samples from a Markov chain from one learning step to the next in order to avoid burning in a Markov chain as part of the inner loop of learning. The procedure is formally presented in Algorithm 1.
  2. why we transform G to minimize log(1-D(G(z))) to G to maximum log(D(G(x)))?
    • Because it provides much strong gradients in early learning.
